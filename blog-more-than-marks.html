More Than Marks: Redefining Success for Today's Children

We built an entire system around one number, and somewhere along the way convinced ourselves it tells us who a child is going to become.

Resilio Foundation July 18, 2026 7 min read

Ask any parent which they'd rather have: a child who brings home straight A's but panics at the thought of losing them, or a child with a B average who's genuinely curious about the world. Most will hesitate before answering, and that hesitation is the whole problem in miniature.

We've built an entire system around one number, an average, a percentage, a rank, and somewhere along the way convinced ourselves it tells us who a child is going to become.

It doesn't. It never really did.

The Weight Kids Are Carrying

Academic pressure shows up early these days, sometimes as early as primary school, and it rarely lets up. Every test becomes a referendum on a child's worth, every report card a verdict rather than a snapshot. Ask a fourteen-year-old what happens if they get a C, and a lot of them won't describe disappointment. They'll describe fear, of their parents, of falling behind, of somehow being less than the kid who got an A.

That fear doesn't make children work harder in any way worth having. Mostly it makes them anxious, secretive about struggling, and less willing to try anything they might not immediately be good at. A system built entirely around marks ends up punishing curiosity, because curiosity involves being bad at something before you're good at it.

Who a Child Is, Beyond the Number

Grades measure a narrow band of ability. They say almost nothing about the parts of a child that actually shape how their life goes. Whether they're kind. Whether they notice when a friend is struggling. Whether they can sit with a hard problem without giving up on it.

Some kids are gifted with numbers. Others are gifted at reading a room, or building something with their hands, or making people feel comfortable in an uncomfortable moment. None of this shows up on a report card.

Creativity Doesn't Fit on a Rubric

Creativity is one of the hardest things to grade, which is probably why schools have historically graded it least. But it's also one of the clearest predictors of how someone handles a world that doesn't hand them a clear set of instructions. The kid who comes up with an unusual answer, or asks a strange question nobody else thought of, is doing something a standardized test was never built to reward.

Steven Spielberg had a C average through school and was rejected from film school three separate times before ever picking up a professional camera.

None of that rejection measured what he was actually capable of. It measured how well he fit a system that had no category for what he'd eventually become.

Character Is Still Being Built, Even When No One's Grading It

Every day at school is teaching a child something about character, whether or not it's on the syllabus. How to handle losing a game. How to apologize when they've hurt someone. Whether it's okay to admit they don't understand something. These lessons happen in hallways and lunch lines far more than they happen in a classroom, and they matter just as much as anything written on a report card.

Emotional Intelligence, the Skill Nobody Tests For

Daniel Goleman's 1995 book made a simple but disruptive argument that reshaped how a lot of people thought about success: the ability to understand and manage your own emotions, and to read other people's, predicts far more about someone's life than IQ ever did.

📖
Emotional Intelligence: Why It Can Matter More Than IQ
Daniel Goleman, 1995

Argues that self-awareness, empathy, and emotional regulation predict success in life more reliably than raw intellect.

Schools have been slow to catch up to that idea, even though most teachers could tell you which kids in their class have it and which don't, long before any test would show them.

A child with strong emotional intelligence knows how to calm down before reacting. They can tell when a friend needs space versus needs company. These are learnable, teachable skills, and a system obsessed with academic output tends to leave them almost entirely to chance.

Growth Mindset: What You Believe About Yourself Matters

Psychologist Carol Dweck's research drew a distinction that changed how a lot of educators think about ability. Some children come to believe intelligence is fixed: you either have it or you don't. Others come to believe it can be developed through effort.

📖
Mindset: The New Psychology of Success
Carol Dweck

A fixed mindset treats a bad grade as proof of a limit. A growth mindset treats it as information about what to work on next.

That single belief, what Dweck calls a growth mindset, changes how a child responds to failure. A fixed mindset treats a bad grade as proof of a limit. A growth mindset treats it as information about what to work on next.

Purpose Matters More Than a Percentile

A child who understands why something matters to them will push through difficulty in a way that a child chasing a number simply won't.

Purpose doesn't need to be grand or fully formed at age twelve. It can be as simple as loving how it feels to solve a puzzle, or caring about animals enough to want to understand biology.

Kids with some sense of purpose tend to be more resilient, not because they're naturally tougher, but because they have a reason to keep going that isn't just "avoid disappointing someone."

Confidence Built on More Than a Score

A child whose entire sense of self rests on their grades is standing on fairly shaky ground, because grades fluctuate, and eventually, for almost everyone, a hard year comes along.

Confidence that's built more broadly, on effort, on specific skills, on relationships, holds up better under pressure than confidence borrowed entirely from a report card.

Wellbeing Is the Foundation, Not the Reward

Student wellbeing often gets treated as something a child earns after they've handled the "real" work of school, a nice extra to fit in if there's time left over. It should be the other way around.

📖
Flourish — the PERMA model of wellbeing
Martin Seligman, 2011

Five elements: Positive emotion, Engagement, Relationships, Meaning, and Accomplishment. None stands in for the others.

A person can feel plenty of joy and still be missing meaning, or feel deeply connected to others but never experience real accomplishment. Wellbeing, in this model, is the sum of all five, not just one good feeling standing in for the rest.

Wellbeing isn't a reward for doing school right. It's what makes doing school possible at all.

Looking Beyond Marks

None of this means grades are meaningless.

They're useful information, one data point among many. The problem is when they become the only one, when a child starts to believe their worth is a number that updates every semester.

Beyond marks is really where most of a child's actual future gets decided, in the habits, character, and self-belief that never show up on a report card but shape everything else that comes after it.

The report card measures a moment. It was never built to measure a person.
